The Wire

A founder I work with called me last month in a panic. She'd been showing investors a cap table that said she owned 55% of her company. After three SAFEs converted at different valuations, she owned 31%. The spreadsheet she'd been using for 18 months was fiction. This issue is about how that happens and what you can do about it before your next raise.

The Deep Dive: Your Cap Table Is Lying to You

Most founders think about their cap table the way most people think about their credit score. They check it once, feel good about the number, and go back to doing something more interesting.

Here's the problem. Your credit score updates automatically. Your cap table doesn't. Every time you issue a SAFE, a convertible note, or a promise-on-a-napkin to an early advisor, you're adding an instrument that lives outside the spreadsheet. It doesn't show up until conversion. And when it converts, the number you thought you owned turns into something else entirely.

I've seen this play out dozens of times. The details change. The pattern never does.

The anatomy of a lie

Let me walk you through how it works. Three acts, like any good disaster.

Act 1: Seed day. Two founders. They split 50/50 because they're equal partners and equals share equally. They set aside a 10% option pool for future hires. The cap table says:

  • Founder A: 45%
  • Founder B: 45%
  • Option pool: 10%

Clean. Simple. Everyone's happy.

Act 2: The SAFEs. Over the next 12 months, they raise $400K from three angel investors. One SAFE at a $3M cap. One at a $5M cap. One at a $4M cap with a 20% discount. None of these show up on the cap table because SAFEs don't convert until a priced round. So the spreadsheet still reads 45/45/10. The founders still tell people they own 90%.

They own nothing of the sort.

Act 3: Series A. A VC offers to lead a $2M round at a $10M pre-money valuation for 20%. Standard stuff. Everyone celebrates the valuation. Nobody models the conversion.

Here's what the cap table actually looks like after the SAFEs convert and the new round closes:

  • Founder A: 26%
  • Founder B: 26%
  • Angel 1 (SAFE at $3M cap): 8.5%
  • Angel 2 (SAFE at $5M cap): 4%
  • Angel 3 (SAFE at $4M cap + discount): 6.5%
  • Option pool (expanded to 15% per term sheet): 15%
  • Series A investor: 14%

Combined founder ownership: 52%. Wait. Where did the other 38% go?

Nowhere. It was always going to end up here. The founders just never modelled it.

Why this keeps happening

There are exactly three reasons cap tables lie, and they're always the same.

Reason 1: SAFEs are invisible. The name is a masterclass in marketing. "Simple Agreement for Future Equity." Every word is reassuring. None of them are accurate. A SAFE is a promise to issue shares at a future date, at a price that depends on conditions that haven't happened yet. Until it converts, it doesn't appear on your cap table. So you issue one, then another, then a third, and your spreadsheet keeps telling you a story that stopped being true months ago.

Reason 2: Nobody models scenarios. I ask every founder the same question: "What does your cap table look like if you raise your Series A at $8M instead of $12M?" The usual answer is a long pause followed by "I'll ask my lawyer." Your lawyer should have already shown you this. If they haven't, they're doing paperwork, not architecture.

Reason 3: The option pool shuffle. This is the one that bothers me most because it's not an accident. It's a negotiating tactic.

Here's how it works. The VC says they want a 15% option pool "post-money." Sounds reasonable. But they insist the pool comes from the founders' shares, not the new investment. So before the round closes, the founders dilute themselves to create the pool. The VC's 20% is calculated after the dilution. The founders pay for the pool. The VC doesn't.

It's perfectly legal. It's standard practice. And nobody explains it in plain language until after the documents are signed.

The fix

I'm not going to tell you it's complicated. It's not. It's maths. The problem is that nobody does the maths until the moment it hurts.

Step 1: Model your SAFEs. Right now. Today. Open a spreadsheet. Put in every SAFE you've issued with its cap, discount, and investment amount. Then model conversion at three different valuation scenarios: your dream valuation, a realistic one, and the worst case.

If you can't do this yourself, that's fine. But you need someone who can, and you need to see the output before your next investor conversation.

Step 2: Stop issuing SAFEs without a running total. Every time you issue a new SAFE, update the model. If the next SAFE pushes your combined founder ownership below 50% at any reasonable conversion scenario, stop and think about whether you actually need to raise more this way.

Step 3: Understand the option pool before you negotiate. When a term sheet says "15% option pool," ask: pre-money or post-money? Who bears the dilution? What's the pool size relative to your current team and hiring plan? If the pool is bigger than you need, you're giving away equity for nothing.

Step 4: Get a cap table tool. Carta, Pulley, Ledgy, whatever works in your jurisdiction. Not a spreadsheet. A tool that models conversion scenarios and shows you the real numbers. A tool that can't be wrong the way a spreadsheet can be wrong when someone forgets to update a formula.

This isn't about paranoia. It's about not being the founder who discovers they own 31% of their company the week their round is supposed to close.

The Blueprint

Cap Table Truth Check: 5 Questions to Ask Right Now

1. What's my fully diluted ownership? Not your "current" ownership. Your ownership after every SAFE, note, and option converts. If you don't know this number, everything below is urgent.

2. At what valuation does my ownership drop below 50%? Model it. If that number is lower than your expected Series A valuation, you have a problem that needs solving before you talk to investors.

3. Who has pro-rata rights? Every investor with pro-rata can invest in your next round and maintain their percentage. This means less room for new investors and more dilution pressure on you.

4. When was the last time my lawyer showed me a conversion waterfall? If the answer is "never" or "what's a conversion waterfall," change lawyers or get a venture architect.

5. Does my cap table tool match my legal documents? Pull up Carta (or whatever you use) and your shareholders' agreement side by side. If the numbers don't match, one of them is wrong. Find out which one.
